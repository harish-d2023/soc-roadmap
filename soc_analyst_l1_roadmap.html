How to Use This Roadmap
Each section in the sidebar corresponds to a skill domain. Within every SIEM or tool section, labs are organized from Beginner → Intermediate → Advanced. You do not need to complete every lab before moving forward — aim for depth in at least two SIEM tools and breadth across the rest. The Home Lab section is your prerequisite for all other labs. Start there, then follow the numbered phases above in order.
Home Lab Setup
Your home lab is your proving ground. Three tiers exist — Basic, Intermediate, and Advanced — each building on the last. You don't need expensive hardware; a modern laptop with 16 GB RAM is sufficient for Tier 1 and 2.
Tier 1 — Basic Lab (Start Here)
Single-Machine Virtualization Lab
Beginner
Set up a virtualization environment on your personal computer to host multiple operating systems simultaneously. This forms the backbone of all future labs.
VirtualBox or VMware Workstation Player Kali Linux ISO Ubuntu Server ISO Windows 10/11 ISO
You learn to create, snapshot, and manage virtual machines. You'll understand NAT vs. Host-Only vs. Bridged networking, which is essential for isolating attack simulations safely.
Log Generation & Collection Baseline
Beginner
Configure your VMs to generate and forward system logs (Windows Event Logs, syslog) to a central collection point — the very foundation of how SIEM tools work.
Windows Event Viewer Syslog-ng or rsyslog Winlogbeat Notepad++ (log inspection)
You gain a working understanding of log sources, log formats (JSON, XML, CEF), and how data flows from endpoints to a collector — the mental model that makes SIEM work intuitive.
Tier 2 — Intermediate Lab
Multi-VM Attack-Defend Network
Intermediate
Build a small internal network with an attacker machine (Kali), a Windows victim, and a Linux server. Simulate basic attacks and observe the logs they generate — the core SOC analyst workflow.
Kali Linux (attacker) Metasploitable 2 or DVWA pfSense (virtual firewall) Wireshark Nmap
You experience alert generation from the defender's perspective — watching how an Nmap scan, a brute-force attempt, or a web exploit appears in logs. This builds the intuition that separates a skilled analyst from a tool operator.
SIEM Integration Lab
Intermediate
Deploy a lightweight SIEM (Wazuh or ELK) within your Tier 2 lab and connect your attack-defend machines to it. Run simulated attacks and observe alerts inside the SIEM dashboard.
Wazuh Server Wazuh Agent (on Windows/Linux VMs) Filebeat Elastic Kibana
You'll correlate raw logs with SIEM-generated alerts for the first time — a milestone moment. You develop the mental map of "what happened on the endpoint" vs. "what the SIEM tells me happened."
Tier 3 — Advanced Lab
Full SOC Simulation Environment
Advanced
Build a production-like SOC environment with a full SIEM, ticketing system, threat intelligence feeds, and simulated corporate network using multiple VLANs. This mirrors what you'll encounter on the job.
Proxmox VE (bare-metal hypervisor) Splunk Free (or Wazuh) TheHive (ticketing / case management) MISP (threat intel) Suricata (IDS/IPS) Active Directory (Windows Server) Atomic Red Team
You can now simulate enterprise attack scenarios end-to-end — from initial compromise to detection, alert triage, ticket creation, escalation, and containment. This lab is your portfolio centerpiece and your interview story.
Purple Team & Threat Hunting Lab
Advanced
Extend the Tier 3 lab with purple teaming capabilities — you play both attacker and defender, using attack simulation frameworks to generate realistic threats and then hunt for them proactively in your SIEM.
Caldera (MITRE ATT&CK automation) Atomic Red Team MITRE ATT&CK Navigator Sigma Rules Splunk or ELK
You learn to map attacker TTPs to MITRE ATT&CK, write detection rules, and validate them against real simulated attack data. This is the skill that gets L1 analysts promoted to L2.
Core Fundamentals
Before touching any SIEM, you need a bedrock of networking, operating systems, and security concepts. These aren't optional prerequisites — they're what lets you understand WHY alerts fire, not just that they fired.
Networking Essentials
Packet Analysis Basics with Wireshark
Beginner
Capture and analyze live and sample network traffic. Learn to read TCP handshakes, HTTP requests, DNS lookups, and identify suspicious patterns in PCAP files.
WiresharkSample PCAP files (Malware Traffic Analysis)
You can dissect a packet capture and identify communication patterns, anomalies, and potential indicators of compromise — a daily SOC skill.
Network Protocol Deep Dive
Intermediate
Study and simulate traffic for protocols that appear most in SOC alerts: DNS (tunneling), HTTP/S (C2 beaconing), SMB (lateral movement), and ICMP (reconnaissance). Understand what normal looks like so you can spot abnormal.
WiresharktcpdumpNmapScapy (Python)
You develop a mental baseline for what "healthy" network traffic looks like — the single most valuable skill for reducing false positive alert fatigue.
Operating System Fundamentals
Windows Event Log Investigation
Beginner
Navigate Windows Event Viewer and identify the most security-relevant Event IDs: 4624 (logon), 4625 (failed logon), 4688 (process creation), 4663 (file access), 7045 (service install). Understand what each tells you.
Windows VMEvent ViewerGet-WinEvent (PowerShell)
You can read and interpret Windows event logs without a SIEM — the raw material behind most endpoint alerts you'll triage.
Linux System Hardening & Log Review
Intermediate
Explore Linux log files (/var/log/auth.log, syslog, audit.log), understand PAM authentication logs, cron job abuse, and sudo privilege escalation patterns. Practice basic hardening steps.
Ubuntu Server VMauditdgrep / awk / sed
You become comfortable with Linux CLI log analysis — critical since most servers in enterprise environments run Linux and generate the bulk of infrastructure logs in a SIEM.
Security Concepts
MITRE ATT&CK Framework Navigation
Beginner
Learn to navigate the ATT&CK matrix. For a given technique (e.g., T1059 — Command and Scripting Interpreter), identify detection opportunities, relevant data sources, and real-world threat actor usage.
MITRE ATT&CK Navigator (web)attack.mitre.org
You can map an alert or suspicious event to an ATT&CK technique, understand its place in an attack chain, and know what to look for next — the basis of threat-informed defense.
Indicators of Compromise (IOC) Analysis
Intermediate
Learn to identify, enrich, and pivot on IOCs — malicious IPs, domains, file hashes, and URLs — using open-source threat intelligence platforms. Practice the analyst workflow of receiving an alert and enriching it with context.
VirusTotalAbuseIPDBShodanMISPAlienVault OTX
You can take a raw IOC from an alert (e.g., an IP address) and within minutes determine whether it's malicious, which threat actors use it, and what action to recommend — core L1 triage competency.

Wazuh Labs

Intermediate
3
Custom Detection Rules in Wazuh

ELK Stack Labs
The ELK Stack (Elasticsearch, Logstash, Kibana) — now called the Elastic Stack — is the open-source foundation under many enterprise SIEM products, including Wazuh and IBM QRadar Insights. Understanding ELK gives you transferable skills across the entire Elastic ecosystem.

1

Beginner
Install Elasticsearch, Logstash, and Kibana on an Ubuntu Server VM. Understand how each component works: Elasticsearch as a data store, Logstash as a pipeline, and Kibana as the visualization layer.
Elasticsearch 8.xLogstashKibanaUbuntu Server VM (8 GB RAM)
You understand the ELK architecture deeply — knowledge that transfers to Wazuh, Elastic SIEM, and any Elastic-based product you encounter professionally.
2
Log Ingestion with Filebeat and Winlogbeat
Beginner
Configure Filebeat on a Linux VM and Winlogbeat on a Windows VM to ship logs into Elasticsearch. Use pre-built dashboards in Kibana to explore the incoming data.
FilebeatWinlogbeatKibana dashboardsWindows + Linux VMs

Intermediate
3
Logstash Pipeline Engineering
Intermediate
Write Logstash pipeline configurations with grok patterns to parse unstructured log formats (Apache access logs, custom application logs) into structured fields that Elasticsearch can index and Kibana can visualize.
LogstashGrok Debugger (Kibana built-in)Sample Apache logs
You can take any raw log format and turn it into structured, searchable data — a practical data engineering skill that SOC engineers use constantly.

Elastic Security — SIEM View and Detection Rules

IBM QRadar Labs
QRadar is deeply embedded in large enterprises, banking, government, and critical infrastructure. It uses a unique offense-based model that groups related events into high-confidence incidents — very different from alert-by-alert SIEM models. Learning QRadar expands your enterprise employability significantly.
Beginner
1
QRadar Community Edition — Setup and Navigation
Beginner
Download and deploy IBM QRadar Community Edition (free, 50 EPS limit). Navigate the key tabs: Dashboard, Offenses, Log Activity, Network Activity, and Assets. Understand the QRadar flow data model vs. log-based SIEMs.
QRadar Community Edition (OVA)14 GB RAM recommendedQRadar documentation (IBM Docs)
You understand QRadar's core architecture — the event pipeline, flow processor, and offense engine — and can orient yourself in its interface without feeling lost.
2
Log Source Integration and DSM Configuration
Beginner
Configure QRadar to receive syslog events from a Linux VM and Windows events via WinCollect. Explore Device Support Module (DSM) parsing and how QRadar normalizes events from different sources into a common taxonomy.
QRadar CEWinCollect AgentLinux syslog VMQRadar DSM Editor
You understand event normalization — how QRadar makes sense of varied log formats — and can onboard new log sources, a frequent task in enterprise SOC deployments.
Intermediate
3
Working with Offenses — Triage and Investigation
Intermediate
Use the QRadar Offense tab to triage auto-generated offenses. For each offense, identify the contributing events, pivot to raw logs, map to MITRE ATT&CK, and practice writing offense notes — exactly as you'd do in an L1 shift.
QRadar CE with sample dataIBM QRadar SIEM Use Cases guideATT&CK Navigator
You can triage a QRadar offense confidently — understanding what triggered it, what events support it, and whether to close, escalate, or investigate further.
4
AQL Queries for Custom Investigations
Intermediate
Learn AQL (Ariel Query Language), QRadar's SQL-like query interface. Write queries against both event and flow data to answer investigative questions: who logged in from an unusual country, which internal hosts initiated external connections on port 443 at odd hours.
QRadar Log Activity tabQRadar AQL reference guideQRadar CE with ingested data
You can move beyond prebuilt rules and conduct custom investigations with AQL — the QRadar equivalent of SPL in Splunk and EQL in Elastic.
Advanced
5
Building Custom Correlation Rules and Use Cases
Advanced
Build custom correlation rules in QRadar's rule engine to detect a multi-stage attack: reconnaissance → initial access → privilege escalation, using event sequences across multiple log sources. Test rule logic with simulated events.
QRadar Rules Engine (offense rules, event rules)Building BlocksReference SetsSample attack data
You understand QRadar's correlation logic at the engineering level — building multi-step detection rules with chained conditions. This is advanced SIEM engineering practiced even at senior L2/L3 levels, and sets you far apart as an L1.

Certifications
Certifications validate your skills to hiring managers who can't see your home lab directly. Choose certifications strategically — prioritize those most recognized by Indian employers and global MSSPs. You don't need all of these; pick two or three that align with your target roles.
Entry Level (Take These First)
🛡️
CompTIA Security+
The most universally recognized baseline security certification. Validates foundational security knowledge across domains. Frequently listed as a minimum requirement in SOC job postings globally and in India's MNC segment.
📊
Splunk Core Certified User
Free study resources available. Validates SPL query skills directly relevant to SOC work. Given Splunk's dominance in enterprise deployments, this is one of the highest-ROI certifications for an L1 analyst candidate.
🎯
Google Cybersecurity Certificate (Coursera)
Low cost, high quality, beginner-friendly. Covers SOC fundamentals, SIEM basics, Python for security, and incident response. A strong starting point before pursuing Security+.
Intermediate (High Value for L1 Roles)
🔬
Blue Team Labs Online / TryHackMe SOC Level 1 Path
Completion certificates from BTLO and THM are widely recognized in the community and demonstrate hands-on skills. The THM SOC Level 1 path directly mirrors the L1 analyst job description and is excellent portfolio evidence.
☁️
SC-200: Microsoft Security Operations Analyst
Validates skills in Microsoft Sentinel, MDE, and Defender XDR — directly tied to the most in-demand cloud SOC platform globally. Highly valued by IT services and consulting firms in Bengaluru.
⚡
CompTIA CySA+ (Cybersecurity Analyst)
The most specific certification to the SOC analyst role at the intermediate level. Covers threat intelligence, vulnerability management, incident response, and SIEM usage in a way that aligns precisely with L1-to-L2 career growth.
Advanced (Longer-Term Goals)
🏆
CEH (Certified Ethical Hacker) — v13
Well-recognized in India's industry and government sectors. Demonstrates understanding of attacker techniques — making you a better defender. Given your existing SuprMentr ethical hacking background, this is a natural progression.
🔐
GCIA (GIAC Certified Intrusion Analyst)
Prestigious, hands-on, and highly respected. Validates advanced network analysis and intrusion detection skills. Best pursued after 12-18 months of practical experience or lab work.
Portfolio & Job Preparation
Technical skill gets you the interview. Your portfolio gets you the offer. An L1 analyst portfolio should demonstrate that you can actually do the job — not just that you studied for it. Here's how to build and present it compellingly.
Portfolio Projects to Build
Home Lab Write-up (Your Cornerstone Project)
Portfolio

Interview Preparation
Technical Interview Topics to Master
The most common L1 analyst interview questions fall into five categories. For each, you should be able to answer from your own lab experience rather than textbook definitions.
Log analysis walkthroughs Alert triage scenarios (true vs false positive) MITRE ATT&CK technique identification Incident response process (Identify → Contain → Eradicate → Recover) Tool-specific SPL/KQL/AQL queries Network protocol analysis (pcap walkthrough)
Every answer should reference a real lab or project. "In my home lab, when I simulated an SSH brute-force, here's what I saw in Wazuh and here's how I would triage it" is exponentially more compelling than "brute-force means repeated login attempts."
